  • The Election Process

    Step 1: The Prerequisites

    So, you want to run for President of the United States? Well before you can even think about running, you have to fulfill a couple requirements. You have to be:

    1. A natural born citizen of the United States

    2. At least 35 years old

    3. A resident of the United States for at least 14 years

    4. A dank memer

    If you fulfill all of these, you can declare your intention to run and move on to the next step to becoming President!

    Step 2: Primaries and Caucuses

    Before you compete in the general election, you will most likely have to go through many state primaries and caucuses in order to be the candidate for your party.

    Primaries

    Primaries are votes held by the state and local governments in which the people choose a candidate they wish to run in the general election for a particular political party. The votes are cast by a secret ballot.

    Caucuses

    Caucuses are similar to primaries, as they serve the same purpose, but they are different in a couple ways. For one, they are private meetings held by the political parties. The participants physically divide themselves into groups to 'vote' for a particular candidate, and they can give speeches in order to persuade others to switch. In the end, the number of people in each group is counted and the results are tallied.

    Delegates

    Delegates are essentially what you are competing for during the state primaries and caucuses. You need to receive a certain amount of delegates (roughly 2400 for Democrats and 1250 for Republicans) in order to secure the nomination from your party. Each primary or caucus awards you delegates based on the people's vote. For Democrats, the amount of delegates you receive from a primary or caucus is usually proportional to the percentage of the vote you received. For Republicans, however, it may be a winner takes all system, a proportional system, or a hybrid of the two.
